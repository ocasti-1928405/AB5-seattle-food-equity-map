Understanding Food Equity

The intersection of geography, economics, and access to nutritious food

What Are Food Deserts?

The term "food desert" describes geographic areas where residents have limited access to affordable and nutritious food, particularly fresh fruits and vegetables. The USDA defines food deserts using two key metrics:

  • Low Income: Census tracts where the poverty rate is 20% or higher, or median family income is at or below 80% of the area median
  • Low Access: At least 500 people or 33% of the population live more than 1 mile (urban) or 10 miles (rural) from the nearest supermarket

While this framework has been valuable for identifying underserved areas, it treats all supermarkets equally—assuming that proximity to any store solves the access problem. Our project challenges this assumption.

The Problem with Proximity-Only Models

Standard food desert mapping focuses solely on distance to stores, missing a critical dimension: affordability. Consider these scenarios:

Scenario A

A low-income neighborhood has three supermarkets within walking distance, but all are premium chains with prices 30-50% higher than budget alternatives. Is this true food access?

Scenario B

A census tract is classified as "food secure" because it has a Whole Foods nearby, yet the median household income is $35,000 and residents rely heavily on emergency food services. What does "access" really mean here?

These examples illustrate why we need to look beyond proximity and consider economic accessibility. A store you cannot afford to shop at is not truly "accessible" food.

Our Price-Adjusted Approach

To address these limitations, we manually classified Seattle's grocery stores and convenience stores into three price tiers:

Budget ($)

Examples: WinCo Foods, Food Depot, local ethnic grocery stores, discount chains

Characteristics: Lowest prices, bulk options, limited prepared foods, may have fewer "premium" brands but provide essential nutrition at accessible price points

Mid-Range ($$)

Examples: Safeway, Fred Meyer, QFC, regional chains

Characteristics: Moderate prices, balance of store brands and national brands, regular sales, loyalty programs, diverse product selection

Premium ($$$)

Examples: Whole Foods, PCC Community Markets, Metropolitan Market

Characteristics: Highest prices, emphasis on organic/specialty items, extensive prepared foods, premium brands, specialized dietary options

Key Findings

By layering our price-tier classification with income data from the American Community Survey, several patterns emerge:

  1. Economic Deserts vs. Geographic Deserts: Some areas technically have food access by proximity measures but lack affordable options relative to local incomes.
  2. Emergency Food Correlation: Neighborhoods with higher ratios of premium to budget stores often show higher density of emergency food providers, suggesting that market failure creates dependency on safety-net services.
  3. Transit-Dependent Populations: For residents without cars, distance to affordable options matters even more. A mile walk carrying groceries is very different from a mile drive.
  4. Cultural Food Access: Budget stores often include ethnic grocers that provide culturally relevant foods at lower prices—an aspect entirely missing from standard food desert metrics.

Data Sources & Methodology

Datasets Used:

  • USDA Food Access Research Atlas (2019): Provides Low Income / Low Access (LILA) classifications for census tracts
  • Seattle Active Business License Data: Filtered by NAICS codes 445110 (Supermarkets) and 445120 (Convenience Stores)
  • US Census TIGER/Line Shapefiles (2010): Geographic boundaries matched to USDA methodology
  • Seattle Neighborhood Boundaries: For intuitive geographic reference
  • Seattle Emergency Food Providers: Locations of food banks and meal programs
  • American Community Survey 5-Year Estimates: Income and demographic data

Classification Methodology:

Price tier classifications were assigned through systematic research of each chain's market positioning, typical product prices compared to Consumer Price Index data, and community perception. Independent grocers were evaluated individually based on available pricing information and product offerings.

Limitations & Future Work

While our approach adds important nuance to food access mapping, we acknowledge several limitations:

  • Price classifications are categorical and don't capture within-store variation or temporal price changes
  • We don't account for store size, product variety, or hours of operation
  • Transportation barriers beyond distance (e.g., bus routes, travel time) aren't fully represented
  • The analysis is static—food access patterns change over time as stores open or close

Future iterations could incorporate dynamic pricing data, transit network analysis, store quality metrics, and temporal changes to provide an even richer picture of food equity.
